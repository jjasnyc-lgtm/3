02 — Project Overview

What This Project Is About

This midterm presents a comprehensive redesign proposal for Montefiore Square, a triangular plaza located at E 138th St and Willis Ave in East Harlem. Despite sitting at a high-traffic intersection surrounded by residential buildings, schools, and local businesses, the plaza currently functions as little more than a concrete pass-through with no meaningful amenity for the community it serves.

The project combines field observation, NYC DOT precedent research, and AI-generated visualization to develop a design proposal that addresses the plaza's core failures: lack of shade and seating, poor pedestrian safety, no community programming, and an unwelcoming spatial character.

Informed by successful DOT plaza interventions — including Westchester Sq Plaza, Myrtle/Wyckoff Plaza, and Hillel Place — this redesign demonstrates that small urban spaces can dramatically improve daily quality of life when designed with real users in mind.

🎯

Essential Question

How can we redesign Montefiore Square to become a safe, welcoming, and active hub for all East Harlem residents?

👥

Primary Users

Seniors, families, youth, daily commuters, and local residents who currently avoid or rapidly pass through the space.

📐

Project Track

Community wellness and pedestrian safety — with secondary focus on green infrastructure and inclusive programming.

🛠️

Methods Used

Site observation via Google Maps, NYC DOT research, iterative AI prompting with DALL·E, and design synthesis.

03 — Site Analysis

Understanding Montefiore Square

A thorough understanding of the site's existing conditions — its movement patterns, social dynamics, physical qualities, and safety context — is essential before proposing any redesign. The following observations were made through Google Street View analysis, aerial mapping, and NYC DOT data.

  • 🚶

    Pedestrian Movement & Desire Lines

    High foot traffic occurs during morning and evening commute hours as residents travel to the nearby 138th St/Grand Concourse subway station. People use the plaza as a diagonal cut-through, but no current paving accommodates this natural desire line — forcing pedestrians onto edges uncomfortably close to traffic. Informal wear patterns in the existing surface reveal where the real paths should be.

  • 🌡️

    Microclimate & Heat Island Effect

    The plaza is almost entirely paved in dark asphalt and concrete, creating a strong heat island effect. With no tree canopy or overhead shade structure, surface temperatures in summer can exceed air temperature by 20–30°F. This makes the space unusable during peak summer heat — precisely when public space is most needed for outdoor relief and community gathering.

  • 🚗

    Vehicle Conflict & Safety Conditions

    Multiple vehicle turning movements cross pedestrian paths with poor sight lines and no raised treatments. Crossing signals are timed for traffic flow, not pedestrian comfort. No protective buffer planting or parking lane separates pedestrians from moving vehicles — especially dangerous for seniors, children, and people with mobility aids.

  • 🏘️

    Surrounding Community Context

    Within a 3-block radius: multi-family residential housing, a public high school, a senior services center, a bodega cluster, and several faith institutions. This diverse mix demands a multi-generational, multi-use design response — not a single-program space. The plaza can simultaneously serve daily commuters, after-school youth, seniors, and weekend community events.

  • 💡

    Lighting & Evening Activation

    The plaza currently lacks adequate pedestrian-scale lighting. The few overhead street lights serve drivers, not pedestrians. After dark, the space feels unsafe and is abandoned even by those who regularly use it during the day. Any redesign must incorporate layered lighting — bollards, string lights, or illuminated seating elements — to extend activation past sunset.

05 — Design Goals

Five Principles Guiding the Redesign

Every design decision in this proposal is anchored to one or more of the following guiding principles — derived from site analysis, community context, and NYC DOT best practices for urban plaza design.

Goal 01

🚶 Prioritize Pedestrians

Redesign the edge conditions and crossing points to give pedestrians unambiguous priority. Use raised crosswalks, planted buffers, and clear desire-line paths to make movement through the plaza feel safe, direct, and comfortable for all users including those with mobility aids.

Goal 02

🌳 Increase Green & Shade

Plant a minimum of 8 mature canopy trees to reduce the heat island effect and create comfortable outdoor conditions from spring through fall. Add native understory planting, seasonal color, and permeable ground surfaces to increase ecological value and visual richness.

Goal 03

♿ Design for All Abilities

Ensure full ADA compliance throughout — level surfaces, tactile paving at transitions, seating with backs and armrests at appropriate heights, and clear visual contrast at all edges. The redesign must work as well for an 80-year-old with a walker as for a teenager on a skateboard.

Goal 04

📅 Enable Programming

Provide a flexible, level central surface with power access to support weekend markets, community events, cultural performances, and fitness classes. A plaza that can be activated by the community is a plaza that belongs to the community — design the space to invite use, not just permit it.

Goal 05

🎨 Reflect Community Identity

Incorporate design elements that reference East Harlem's cultural heritage — mosaic tile work, warm material palettes, and planting choices that connect to the neighborhood's Latin and African American traditions. The plaza should feel like it belongs to this specific community, not any generic city plaza.

07 — Community Impact

Who This Redesign Serves

A plaza redesign is only successful if it genuinely improves daily life for the people who live and work nearby. This proposal was developed with specific user groups and community needs at the center of every decision — not as an afterthought.

  • 👴

    Seniors & Older Adults

    Multiple accessible seating nodes with backed benches, armrests, and shade coverage create comfortable resting spaces for seniors walking to and from the nearby senior center and subway station. Level surfaces and tactile paving ensure safe navigation for those with mobility aids and visual impairments.

  • 👨‍👩‍👧

    Families with Children

    The central flexible lawn area and shaded seating zones provide a safe gathering space for families — particularly after school and on weekends. The redesign removes the dangerous ambiguity between pedestrian and vehicle space that currently makes the plaza feel unsafe for young children and stroller users.

  • 🧑

    Youth & Students

    The plaza's proximity to a local high school means students pass through or gather here daily. The proposed seating walls, open social space, and flexible event surface give youth a legitimate place to be after school — reducing conflicts and improving the perceived and actual safety of the entire block.

  • 🛒

    Local Vendors & Businesses

    The weekend programming zone with power access supports a rotating community market — providing income opportunities for local vendors while drawing foot traffic that benefits surrounding businesses. A lively, activated plaza is economically beneficial for the neighborhood, not just aesthetically pleasant.

  • 🌍

    The Broader Neighborhood

    Native planting improves local air quality and supports pollinators. Permeable surfaces reduce stormwater runoff. Shade trees mitigate the urban heat island at a neighborhood scale. This redesign delivers environmental benefits that extend far beyond the plaza's physical boundaries.

08 — Reflection & Conclusion

AI as a Design Tool — Not a Designer

This project used DALL·E as a rapid visualization and exploration tool throughout the design process. Reflecting honestly on where AI added value — and where it fell short — is central to understanding how to use these tools responsibly in design practice.

What AI Helped With

AI image generation was most valuable for rapidly visualizing multiple design directions without committing to detailed manual drawing. In a matter of hours, it was possible to explore a green wellness concept, a market plaza, a pedestrian safety focus, and aerial layout studies — generating enough visual material to make informed comparisons between approaches.

AI also excelled at communicating atmosphere — the warmth of afternoon light through a canopy, the social energy of an evening market, the calm of a shaded seating alcove. These qualities are difficult to convey in a hand sketch but critical for presenting a design vision convincingly to reviewers and stakeholders.

Iterative prompting allowed the imagery to become increasingly specific and site-appropriate — moving from generic "urban plaza" outputs to renderings that felt genuinely connected to Harlem's cultural and spatial identity.

What Required Human Judgment

Effective prompting required genuine site knowledge. The most useful images only emerged after studying the actual plaza through Google Maps, NYC DOT references, and site analysis. Generic prompts produced generic results. Every useful output was earned through specific, informed prompting — not from accepting the first result.

Selecting between options required design judgment. AI produced images, but it could not evaluate which direction best served the community's real needs, which was most physically feasible, or which was most culturally appropriate. Those decisions required human understanding of the site, the users, and the design discipline.

All analysis, justification, and written content was entirely human work. The site analysis, goal development, community impact assessment, and design rationale represent original thinking that AI tools cannot replicate — and should not be expected to.

Key Lessons from this Project
  • Start with real site knowledge. The quality of AI outputs is directly proportional to the quality of your site understanding and prompting specificity.
  • Iterate, don't accept the first result. Every useful image in this project came after at least 3–5 prompt refinements — not from the first attempt.
  • Use AI to explore, not to conclude. AI is a tool for rapid exploration. The conclusions, judgments, and design rationale must always be made by the designer.
  • Small spaces matter enormously. A thoughtful plaza redesign can have a massive impact on daily quality of life for thousands of residents at a fraction of the cost of major infrastructure.
  • Design for real people, not ideal users. The most important decisions here came from thinking specifically about seniors, children, and daily commuters — not abstract "users."
